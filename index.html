The problem

Lab stock management is a mess

In shared research labs, consumables pile up in unmarked containers, stock levels exist only in someone's head, and chemicals get reordered weeks before they are actually needed, or not at all until it is too late.

Nobody knows what is where

Chemicals get stored wherever there is space. New people spend hours hunting for things that were there all along, just mislabelled or in a different fridge.

Duplicate orders pile up

Without real-time stock data, the same chemical gets ordered multiple times from different people. Budgets take the hit without anyone noticing until it is too late.

No paper trail for hazardous items

Expensive or regulated chemicals get ordered by whoever asks first, with no record of why, no approval step, and no way to audit who used what.

The solution

How ASS works

For labs where consumable management falls through the cracks, ASS is a barcode-based software system that logs every chemical from the moment it arrives to the moment it runs out, with automatic reordering and an approval workflow for restricted items.
1

Scan and label new arrivals

Scan the supplier barcode when a chemical arrives. The system identifies it automatically. You print a standardized label, add any extra details on the touchscreen, and place the item wherever makes sense.

2

Find anything in seconds

Any lab computer can search the full inventory by name, ID, or chemical category. Location, storage conditions, expiry date, and who last used it are all one search away.

3

Empty container? Scan and confirm.

When stock runs out, scan the label and confirm. The system checks whether the item exists elsewhere in the lab before placing any order. If it is genuinely depleted, the order goes out automatically and both the student and the technician get an email.

4

Restricted items need approval

For expensive or hazardous chemicals, students submit a request through the system instead of ordering directly. The lab technician reviews it and either approves or rejects it. The student gets an email either way, including a reason if the request was turned down.
